Thank you again for the review — every one of your flags turned out to be a real signal. Our first interpretation of them didn't survive verification, so we re-investigated each discrepancy against the original 1000-node run's own logs and monerod source, and then re-ran your exact 1000-node configuration on the fixed stack and analyzed it with your own `xmrpeers` code. Full writeup with measurements, source citations, and reproduction scripts: `docs/20260610_rucknium_review_response_v2.md`. The short version:

**1. The connection-duration gap (your 1.52 min vs mainnet's 23 min) is solved — and your metric was measuring it correctly.**

You wrote *"the cause of the discrepancy is unclear."* It's monerod's own sync-search peer rotation. `update_sync_search()` runs on a 101-second timer (`epee::math_helper::once_a_time_seconds<101>`), and when all 12 outbound slots hold synced peers with fewer than 2 in `state_synchronizing`, it drops the last non-anchor synced out-peer to make room to search for sync candidates. In the simulator every node is always synced and every replacement connection succeeds within ~1 s, so the dropper fires every 101 s without exception and the newest connection is, emergently, the usual victim. The result in our logs: tx-carrying connections die at **median 100.3 s with p25–p90 of 100.0–100.9 s** — exactly one timer period. The `"dropping synced peer, 0 syncing, 12 synced, 12 max out peers"` line fires at 101.001 s median cadence (497 times in 16 h), each followed within 1 ms by the CLOSE of that peer. The 2 **anchor** connections (monero's countermeasure from the Eclipse-attacks paper) are exempt — they're the hours-long survivors behind your 709-minute max.

One mechanism explains all three environments in your review: our 1000-node run (instant refill → one-period tenures → 1.5 min), mainnet (refill friction from stale/unreachable candidates spreads the rotation → ~23 min), and your 35-node run (peerlist exhausted, nothing to rotate to → 120 min, with your 4,079-min max bounded by the 72 h you ran it).

On your method itself: gossip is continuous within connections (median inter-NOTIFY gap 4.9 s), so the tx-gap inference is a good proxy for the TCP lifetime of tx-carrying connections — emulating your method on the same logs reproduces your numbers (1.53 vs your 1.52 min). An earlier draft of ours suggested your metric was a measurement artifact; that was wrong.

*Question for you:* if your mainnet node's logs are handy — do you see `dropping synced peer` lines, and at what cadence relative to your connection count? That would directly confirm the mainnet side of this account.

**2. Your review did lead us to a real bug — but it's orthogonal to everything you measured.**

Chasing the connection-duration flag we found that monerod's default `max-connections-per-ip=1` breaks small, *dense* simulations (a 15-node full-mesh test: 31,180 refused connections across the run, no stable mesh; flooring the cap at 4 fixes it — commit `d21f971b`). At 1000 nodes, however, every measured profile is identical before and after the fix (tx-carrying lifetime 100.3 s in both; handshake success 9.6 % vs 10.0 %; the ~0.4 s connection-attempt churn is normal peer-discovery probing at that scale). So the fix is a genuine correctness improvement we owe to your review — but it does not explain, and did not change, any statistic you measured.

**3. Clumping is volume-bound and saturates — and your v0.1.0 measurement was healthy, reproducible behavior.**

First, a rate correction on our side: runs deliver fewer transactions than configured (wallets wait on funding and confirmations). The 1000-node run you analyzed delivered **0.466 tx/s**, not the configured 0.67. The measured curve (% single-tx messages vs *delivered* tx/s) now has four points: 0.080 → 92.4 %, 0.226 → 49.5 %, 0.345 → 23.0 %, 0.466 → 23.4 % (vs your mainnet 1.45 → 25.05 %). The 0.345 point is the new replication: your exact 1000-node configuration re-run on the fixed stack, completing cleanly, analyzed with your code — **23.03 % singles and connection-duration median 1.5238 min**, within 0.4 pp and within rounding of your originals. So the v0.1.0 numbers were not artifacts of a sick run; they reproduce on a healthy one.

The sim-side mechanism is measured end-to-end: every tx crosses every connection (31.9 receipts per tx ≈ node degree), batched per-connection by the stock Dandelion++ flush timers — the 5 s-in / 2.5 s-out asymmetry is directly visible in the data (received-on-outbound: 9.0 % singles, 4.25 txs/msg, 6.58 s cadence; received-on-inbound: 32.5 %, 2.73, 3.88 s) — plus cascade re-batching that compounds clumps hop over hop, which is why the curve saturates near ~23 % singles.

That leaves the genuinely interesting open question: at your mainnet 1.45 tx/s, this same mechanism would predict ≲10 % singles, yet you measured 25 % — mainnet damps clumping roughly 3× per unit rate relative to the simulator. Back-solving, your node's effective per-connection λ was ≈ 2.4 txs per flush. *Two things only your data can answer:* roughly how many P2P connections did your node hold during that window, and does your log show each tx arriving on (nearly) every connection — i.e., full flooding? Candidates we can't distinguish from here: connection count, NOTIFY size-splitting at high rates, real-network jitter de-synchronizing the flush queues.

**4. The one-second cycle.** The quarter-second grid is real: `fluff_stepsize` is `std::ratio<1,4>` — fluff delays are Poisson-distributed counts of 250 ms ticks. Your latency hypothesis doesn't fit the data, though: your 35-node run and our 1000-node run show the same eighth-second comb under very different latency mixes. Our best account is that the deterministic, jitter-free simulator sharpens fine structure that real-network jitter smears on mainnet — the "subtle double-mode on either side" you noted in your own mainnet plot looks like the same sub-structure. Why specifically *eighth*-second remains open; we won't pretend otherwise.

**5. Skellam.** The original 1000-node run's excess off-grid observations remain unexplained (plausibly flush-phase mixing under the 101 s rotation, unverified); the completing runs fit the theoretical distribution well.

**Where this leaves fidelity, and what we'll do next.** The simulator runs the stock protocol faithfully — the wire census shows only stock levin commands, and the same binary ran every experiment in this series. The connection-tenure gap is *environmental* (a perfect network: 100 % reachable peers, instant handshakes, zero churn), not protocol infidelity. Two changes should close it without touching monerod: (a) **node churn** — monerod suppresses a failed address for a full hour (`P2P_FAILED_ADDR_FORGET_SECONDS`), so even a few %/hour of churn starves slot-refill and stalls the rotation; our process-phase machinery can already schedule stop/restarts; (b) an **unreachable-majority topology** (`--hide-my-port` nodes — which, it turns out, never enter peerlists at all, since white-list insertion is gated on a successful back-ping). Falsifiable prediction: median tx-carrying tenure rises from ~100 s toward tens of minutes. We'll run that A/B.

Honest capability summary: 1000 nodes sustain at least ~0.35–0.47 tx/s delivered on this hardware with full protocol fidelity; spam-wave intensity (1.45 tx/s) at 1000 nodes is untested — reaching it is a user-count question (our per-user delivery saturates), and an earlier claim of ours that it was impossible at this scale was based on a capacity model that direct experiment refuted.

Thanks again — both of your headline statistics turned out to be faithful measurements of real mechanisms, one of which (the 101 s rotation) we would never have found without this review. The archives, the gossip-to-TCP join script, and the analysis outputs are all in the repo if you'd like to re-analyze anything yourself, and we're happy to run whatever else would be useful.
